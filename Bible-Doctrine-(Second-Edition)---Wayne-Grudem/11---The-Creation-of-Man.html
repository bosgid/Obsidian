11 - The Creation of Man

Feb 08, 2024, 18 min read

What was the purpose for which God created man? On that basis, what is to be the primary purpose of our lives? ¬ß

As we have seen in Chapter 4, God does not need anything and there is nothing that He lacks. He also does not fellowship and He is not lonely because there is everlasting fellowship and perfect amongst the members of the Trinity. This is important when we consider the purpose behind God‚Äôs creation of Man. He clearly did not need to create us, but instead He chose to.

God created us for His glory. This is true of all creation, but more specifically for Man (Isa 43:7; cf. Eph 1:11-12). This fact guarantees that our lives are significant. When we first realize that God did not need to create us and does not need us for anything, we could conclude that our lives have no importance at all. But Scripture tells us that we were created to glorify God, indicating that we are important to God himself. This is the final definition of genuine importance or significance to our lives: If we are truly important to God for all eternity, then what greater measure of importance or significance could we want?

This all leads us to understanding of what our purpose is in life: to fulfill the reason that God created us. This purpose is, therefore, to glorify Him. When we are speaking with respect to God Himself, that is a good summary of our purpose. But when we think of our own interests, we make the happy discovery that we are to enjoy God and take delight in him and in our relationship to him (John 10:10; Ps 16:11, Ps 27:4). Fullness of joy is found in knowing God and delighting in the excellence of his character. To be in his presence, to enjoy fellowship with him, is a greater blessing than anything that can be imagined. Therefore, the normal heart attitude of a Christian is rejoicing in the Lord and in the lessons of the life he gives us (Rom 5:2-3; Phil 4:4; 1 Thess 5:16-18; James 1:2; 1 Pet 1:6-8; etc.). As we glorify God and enjoy him, Scripture tells us that he rejoices in us (Isa 62:5; Zeph 3:17).

This understanding of the doctrine of the creation of man has very practical results. When we realize that God created us to glorify him, and when we start to act in ways that fulfill that purpose, then we begin to experience an intensity of joy in the Lord that we have never before known. When we add to that the realization that God himself is rejoicing in our fellowship with him, our joy becomes ‚Äúinexpressible and filled with heavenly glory‚Äù (1 Pet 1:8)

What does it mean to be made ‚Äúin the image of God‚Äù? In what ways is our existence like God‚Äôs? ¬ß

Genesis 1:26-27

  1. Then God said, ‚ÄúLet us make man in our image, after our likeness. And let them have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over the livestock and over all the earth and over every creeping thing that creeps on the earth.‚Äù

  2. So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him; male and female he created them.

The fact that man is in the image of God means that man is like God and represents God.

Both the Hebrew word for ‚Äúimage‚Äù (tselem) and the Hebrew word for ‚Äúlikeness‚Äù (demut) refer to something that is similar but not identical to the thing it represents or is an ‚Äúimage‚Äù of. The word image can also be used of something that represents something else. The words image and likeness had quite clear meanings to the original readers.

When we realize that the Hebrew words for ‚Äúimage‚Äù and ‚Äúlikeness‚Äù simply informed the original readers that man was like God and would in many ways represent God, much of the controversy over the meaning of ‚Äúimage of God‚Äù is seen to be a search for too narrow and too specific a meaning. When Scripture reports that God said, ‚ÄúLet us make man in our image, after our likeness‚Äù (Gen 1:26), it simply would have meant to the original readers, ‚ÄúLet us make man to be like us and to represent us.‚Äù

As we read the rest of Scripture, we realize that a full understanding of man‚Äôs likeness to God would require a full understanding of who God is in his being and in his actions and a full understanding of who man is and what he does. The more we know about God and man the more similarities we will recognize and the more fully we will understand what Scripture means when it says that man is in the image of God. The expression refers to every way in which man is like God.

Though it would be difficult to define all the ways in which we are like God, we can nevertheless mention several aspects of our existence that show us to be more like God than all the rest of creation:

  • Moral aspects

We are creatures who are morally accountable before God for our actions. Corresponding to that accountability, we have an inner sense of right and wrong that sets us apart from animals (who have little if any innate sense of morality or justice but simply respond from fear of punishment or hope of reward). When we act according to God‚Äôs moral standards, our likeness to God is reflected in behavior that is holy and righteous before him, but, by contrast, our unlikeness to God is reflected whenever we sin.

  • Spiritual aspects

We have not only physical bodies but also immaterial spirits, and we can therefore act in ways that are significant in the immaterial, spiritual realm of existence. This means that we have a spiritual life that enables us to relate to God as persons, and we also have immortality; we will not cease to exist, but will live forever.

  • Mental aspects

We have an ability to reason, think logically, and learn that sets us apart from the animal world. Animals sometimes exhibit remarkable behaviour in solving mazes or working out problems in the physical world, but they certainly do not engage in abstract reasoning. It is quite clear that since creation, animals have not developed at all in their understanding of ethical problems or use of philosophical concepts and the like. No group of animals will ever be found debating philosophical, ethical, or theological issues. We innovate and what we create continues to improve and develop, whilst animals will always continue to create the same things with no innovation as time passes (e.g. birds nests, bee hives, beaver dams, etc.). Our complex use of human languages as well as our entire spectrum of human creativity which includes all forms of art, music, literature, and science is also what separates us from the rest of creation. Furthermore, the degree and complexity of human emotions indicate just how vast is the difference between humankind and the rest of creation.

  • Relational aspects

In addition to our unique ability to relate to God, there are other relational aspects of being in God‚Äôs image. Although animals no doubt have some sense of community with each other, the depth of interpersonal harmony experienced in human marriage, in a human family when it functions according to God‚Äôs principles, and in a church when a community of believers is walking in fellowship with the Lord and with one another is much greater than the interpersonal harmony experienced by any animals. Man is like God also in his relationship to the rest of creation. Specifically, man has been given the right to rule over the creation and has even been given the authority when Christ returns to sit in judgment over angels (Gen 1:26-28; Ps 8:6-8; 1 Cor 6:3). This relational aspect is also seen in how man is able to innovate through collaboration as each generation builds on the progress of the previous generation. This kind of collaboration is not seen anywhere else in creation.

What effect did the fall of mankind have on our being made in the image of God? What is God‚Äôs remedy for this? ¬ß

We might wonder whether man could still be thought to be like God after he sinned. This question is answered quite early in Genesis, where God gives Noah the authority to establish the death penalty for murder among human beings just after the flood: God says, ‚ÄúWhoever sheds the blood of man, by man shall his blood be shed, for God made man in his own image‚Äù (Gen 9:6). Even though people are sinful, there is still enough likeness to God remaining in them that to murder another person (i.e. ‚Äúshed blood‚Äù) is to attack the part of creation that most resembles God, and it betrays an attempt or desire (if one were able) to attack God himself. Man is still in God‚Äôs image (cf. James 3:9).

However, since man has sinned, he is certainly not as fully like God as he was before. His moral purity has been lost and his sinful character certainly does not reflect God‚Äôs holiness. His intellect is corrupted by falsehood and misunderstanding his speech no longer continually glorifies God; his relationships are often governed by selfishness rather than love, and so forth. Though man is still in the image of God, in every aspect of life some parts of that image have been distorted or lost. In short, ‚ÄúGod made man upright, but they have sought out many schemes‚Äù (Eccl 7:29). After the fall, then, we are still in God‚Äôs image ‚Äîwe are still like God and we still represent God ‚Äîbut the image of God in us is distorted; we are less fully like God than we were before the entrance of sin.

However, because of Christ‚Äôs work on the cross, we who believe can progressively grow into more and more likeness to God. As we gain in true understanding of God, his Word, and his world, we begin to think more and more of the thoughts that God himself thinks. In this way, we are ‚Äúrenewed in knowledge‚Äù and become more like God in our thinking (Col 3:10; Rom 12:2). This is a description of the ordinary course of the Christian life. Throughout this life, as we grow in Christian maturity, we grow in greater likeness to God. More particularly, we grow in likeness to Christ in our lives and in our character (2 Cor 3:18. In fact, the goal for which God has redeemed us is that we might be ‚Äúconformed to the image of his Son‚Äù (Rom 8:29) and thus be exactly like Christ in our moral character.

The amazing promise of the New Testament is that, just as we have been like Adam (subject to death and sin), we shall also be like Christ (morally pure, never subject to death again) (1 Cor 15:49). The full measure of our creation in the image of God is not seen in the life of Adam who sinned, nor is it seen in our lives now, for we are imperfect. But the New Testament emphasizes that God‚Äôs purpose in creating man in his image was completely realized in the person of Jesus Christ. He himself ‚Äúis the image of God‚Äù (2 Cor 4:4; Col 1:15). In Jesus we see human likeness to God as it was intended to be, and it should cause us to rejoice that God has predestined us ‚Äúto be conformed to the image of his Son‚Äù (Rom 8:29; cf 1 Cor 15:49; 1 John 3:2).

Differentiate between the trichotomist and the dichotomist views of man. ¬ß

Trichotomy

The view that man is made of three parts (body, soul, and spirit) is called trichotomy. According to many trichotomists, man‚Äôs soul includes his intellect, his emotions, and his will. They maintain that all people have such a soul and that the different elements of the soul can either serve God or be yielded to sin. They argue that man‚Äôs spirit is a higher faculty that comes alive when a person becomes a Christian (see Rom 8:10). The spirit of a person, then, would be the part that most directly worships and prays to God (see John 4:24; Phil 3:3).

Dichotomy

This view teaches that ‚Äúspirit‚Äù is not a separate part of man, but simply another term for ‚Äúsoul‚Äù and that both terms are used interchangeably in Scripture to talk about the immaterial part of man, the part that lives on after our bodies die. Therefore, man is made up of two parts (body and soul/spirit). Those who hold this view often agree that Scripture uses the word ‚Äúspirit‚Äù (Heb. ruach and Gk. pneuma) more frequently when referring to our relationship to God, but such usage, they say, is not uniform, and the word soul is also used in all the ways that spirit can be used.

Does there appear to be any difference between Scripture‚Äôs use of the word spirit and its use of the word soul? Explain. ¬ß

This is difficult to answer because when I investigate the relevant passages of Scripture, it appears to me that the answer changes depending on which group of Scripture one looks at. There are cases where there seems to be a clear distinction made which is difficult to explain away without some rather large logical leaps, but then there are also cases where it seems quite apparent that the text is referring to the same thing.

In Scripture, we see a clear emphasis that God created us to have a unity between body and soul. Once this point is firmly established, we can go on to see that Scripture also quite clearly teaches that there is an immaterial part of man‚Äôs nature is ‚Äúsoul‚Äù (Heb. nephesh and Gk. psyche) and ‚Äúspirit‚Äù (Heb. ruach and Gk. pneuma). To find more information on the topic of the immaterial part of man‚Äôs nature, one would need to inspect all the uses of these words in the Bible.

The verses which support trichotomy will be handled in the next question. The verses supporting dichotomy will be handled here in this question:

In John‚Äôs gospel, it appears that a similar context in two different scenarios uses a different word to describe the same thing; i.e. to be ‚Äútroubled in soul/spirit‚Äù (John 12:27 and John 13:21). Similarly, we read of Mary‚Äôs words in Luke 1:46-47 where it seems she uses the two words interchangeably. The interchangeability of terms is also seen when describing people who have died and gone to heaven or hell where they are called either ‚Äúspirits‚Äù (Heb 12:23; 1 Pet 3:19) or ‚Äúsouls‚Äù (Rev 6:9, 20:4).

The part of man that exists after death is also encapsulated by the word ‚Äúsoul‚Äù in parts of Scripture which would lead one to believe that the word ‚Äúsoul‚Äù represents the entire immaterial part of man (Matt 10:28). But the same can be said of the ‚Äúspirit‚Äù in other parts of Scripture which would then lead one to believe that the words can be used interchangeably (1 Cor 5:5; James 2:26).

The use of either word to represent the entirety of man‚Äôs immaterial nature can also be seen in verses where only the word ‚Äúspirit‚Äù is used regarding the topic of completely cleansing ourselves from all forms of defilement, physical or non-physical (2 Cor 7:1; see also 1 Cor 5:3; Col 2:5). However, in this specific case, I can see how one may argue that the defilement of the spirit can come through the soul, which would uphold a trichotomist view without contradicting this verse.

When trying to define the different parts of the trichotomy of man, it can become difficult to assign different functions for each part (body, soul, and spirit) when confronted with verses which seems to show the words ‚Äúspirit‚Äù and ‚Äúsoul‚Äù performing the same activities. The activities of thinking, feeling, and deciding is also said to be done by our spirits which can also experience emotions according to Scripture (Acts 17:16; John 13:21; Prov 17:22). Also, the functions of knowing, perceiving, and thinking are also said to be done by our spirits (Mark 2:8; Rom 8:16, act of bearing witness and understanding; 1 Cor 2:11). These verses could potentially be handled on a one-by-one basis with explanations as to why the use of these words do not imply that the activities are shared between soul and spirit, but for the sake of the argument and the answer to the question, I believe it‚Äôs important to mention them. There is also reference to not only our spirits worshipping God and partaking in spiritual activity, but our souls as well (Ps 25:1, Ps 62:1, Ps 103:1, Ps 146:1; Luke 1:46).

The final point which makes a trichotomist view difficult is the great commandment (Deut 6:5; Mark 12:30). There is no reference here to the spirit of a person and I believe it would be incorrect to say that our spirits have no part of the great commandment. In order to resolve this, we almost have no choice but to agree that the uses of the word ‚Äúsoul‚Äù here represents the spirit as well. This conclusion would also support any other verses used in a similar regard when arguing for a dichotomist view.

List two of the Scripture passages that are used to support the trichotomist position and give the dichotomist response to them. ¬ß

The main verse in question is 1 Thessalonians 5:23. Here there seems to be a clear distinction between body, soul, and spirit. The counter dichotomist response is to say that the words ‚Äúsoul‚Äù and ‚Äúspirit‚Äù are synonyms in this verse and they are repeated for emphasis. This argument is supported when looking at similar cases like the great commandment in Mark 12:30 where it seems repetition of similar words is used to emphasis a point. To deny this is to affirm that Mark 12:30 proves that there are four parts to us which are different to the three in question. This is very likely an incorrect conclusion.

However, even though that is the counter-argument to the trichotomist view of this verse, it is still difficult to see why Paul would do this. It would make more sense for him to use three words if he was intentionally referring to three different parts. This separation also gives way to explaining the use of soul and spirit in the light of it being two differing parts of a person when Mary uses these words in Luke 1:46-47 (this verse was used in the previous question in the argument for dichotomy).

Furthermore, there is also some value in observing how these Scriptures practically play out in our Christian lives. I have left this part of the discussion for last because I do not like using experiences to form arguments instead of Scripture, but I do believe there is some value when you pair them together. An understanding of our spirits being born again while the rest of us (our mind and body) remains the same aligns with Scripture and our experience of salvation. It is clear in Scripture that our rebirth is purely spiritual and is not outwardly visible (John 3:5-6). When we are reborn spiritually, we can stand in confidence on scripture that states that we are a new creation (2 Cor 5:17). Yet, though we are a new creation, we still look the same and still have our same mental and emotional habits. It is true that we can be instantly saved from addictions and sinful desires or thoughts at the point of our salvation, but most commonly we are saved from these things through sanctification. This reality that we face and the verses pertaining to rebirth and a new creation seemingly can only be solved when viewed through a trichotomist lens.

However, one may also argue that the immaterial side of us is still like a baby (1 Cor 3:1-3), therefore, though we are reborn in spirit (i.e. soul), we still need to grow and mature.

Both views have strong arguments and can defend themselves well. The dichotomist view does seem to have more Scriptural backing, but the trichotomist view seems to align more with our current reality as believers based on the verses we read. For now, I am personally uncertain of which of these two views is the ‚Äúcorrect view‚Äù and I currently do not see and urgent need for me to ‚Äúpick a side‚Äù. In some ways I can even affirm that both are true.
