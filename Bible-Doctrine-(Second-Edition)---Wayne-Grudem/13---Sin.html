Define sin. ¬ß

Sin

Sin is any failure to conform to the moral law of God in act, attitude, or nature.

Acts that go against the moral law of God are easy to identify: stealing, lying, murder, adultery, etc. But the definition of sin also extends to our attitudes and desires.

Starting in the Ten Commandments, we see wrong attitudes being prohibited (Exod 20:17). Here God specifies that a desire to steal or to commit adultery is also sin in his sight. The Sermon on the Mount also prohibits sinful attitudes such as anger (Matt 5:22) or lust (Matt 5:28Matt 5:28). Paul also lists attitudes such as jealousy, anger, and rivalries (Gal 5:20) as things that are works of the flesh opposed to the desires of the Spirit (Gal 5:17, 5:19). Therefore, a life that is pleasing to God is one that has moral purity not only in its actions, but also in its desires of heart. This is even seen in the greatest commandment: ‚ÄúYou shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind and with all your strength‚Äù (Mark 12:30).

Also, our very nature, the internal character that is the essence of who we are as persons, can also be sinful. According to Scripture, before we were redeemed by Christ, not only did we do sinful acts and have sinful attitudes, but we were also sinners by nature (Rom 5:8; Eph 2:3).

Explain the term ‚Äúinherited guilt‚Äù How would you respond to the objection that this teaching is unfair? ¬ß

‚ÄúInherited guilt‚Äù means we are counted guilty because of Adam‚Äôs sin. Scripture speaks of sin spreading through to the human race through one man - Adam - in the same way forgiveness is given to all who believe through one man - Jesus Christ (Rom 5:12). According to this passage, ‚Äúall sinned‚Äù (all of mankind) because Adam sinned. The idea that ‚Äúall men sinned‚Äù means that God thought of us all as having sinned when Adam disobeyed is further indicated by the next two verses (Rom 5:13-14) where Paul points out that even before the law was given through Moses, people died. Death comes as a result of sin (Rom 6:23), so the fact that people died is very good proof that God counted people guilty on the basis of Adam‚Äôs sin. Through the trespass of Adam, all of mankind were made sinners (Rom 5:18-19). God sent His Son to die for us while we were sinners (Rom 5:8), but none of us even existed when Christ died, therefore, God looked ahead to the future and even then regarded us as sinners in need of salvation before we were even born.

It is difficult to accept that we are labelled a sinner because of someone else‚Äôs actions, but it is also difficult to claim this as unfair for anyone who would make that claim would have to first admit that they have themselves sinned before. We have a tendency to sin and it is something that everyone does and most of the time unknowingly. So if we sin so easily then is it not fair to be labelled as sinners? Many may admit that they are labelled sinners as a result of their own actions, but struggle to accept that they are sinners because as a result of Adam‚Äôs actions. However, according to the Paul‚Äôs argument and defence in Romans 5, it is of utmost importance that we accept this to be true. According to this passage of Scripture, if we cannot except that we are guilty because of the actions of Adam, then we are not able to accept that we are forgiven because of the actions of Jesus. Therefore, if it is unfair for us to be represented by Adam, then we should also think it is unfair for us to be represented by Christ and to have his righteousness imputed to us by God.

However, the reality is that Adam, our first representative, sinned ‚Äîand God counted us guilty. But Christ, the representative of all who believe in him, obeyed God perfectly-and God counted us righteous. That is simply the way in which God set up the human race to work. God regards the human race as an organic whole, a unity, represented by Adam as its head. And God also thinks of the new race of Christians, those who are redeemed by Christ, as an organic whole, a unity represented by Christ as head of his people.

How did Adam‚Äôs sin affect our own human natures? Does this mean that all people are as bad as they could be? Explain. ¬ß

In addition to the legal guilt that God imputes to us because of Adam‚Äôs sin, we also inherit a sinful nature because of Adam‚Äôs sin. This inherited sinful nature has many terms, but the best fitting one for this context is the term ‚Äúinherited corruption‚Äù because it seems to express more clearly the specific idea in view.

An example of this ‚Äúinherited corruption‚Äù can be seen in Psalms 51, specifically vese 5.

Psalm 51:5

  1. Behold, I was brought forth in iniquity, and in sin did my mother conceive me.

David is so overwhelmed with the consciousness of his own sin that as he looks back on his life he realizes that he was sinful from the beginning. As far back as he can think of himself, he realizes that he has had a sinful nature. Moreover, even before he was born, he had a sinful disposition: He affirms that at the moment of conception he had a sinful nature, for ‚Äúin sin did my mother conceive me‚Äù. Here is a strong statement of the inherent tendency to sin that attaches to our lives from the very beginning. A similar idea is affirmed in Psalm 58:3.

According to Paul, this is the standard nature of all humankind (Eph 2:3). We can even see evidence of this when observing children even from an extremely young age who already start to display selfish and sinful tendencies. Because of the Fall, all of mankind have inherited a tendency within themselves towards sin. This is our natural inclination when left to our own devices. Even without knowledge of the Bible, many have come to the same conclusion. Yet we know it is some inherited by from Adam and not from God because God originally created man ‚Äúgood‚Äù and that all changed after Adam sinned and was cursed. So if God intended us to be created good, yet we are all born with this skill and tendency for sinning, then we can only conclude that this corrupted nature did not stop with Adam, but has continued and been inherited by all of mankind.

This corruption goes so deep that, without Christ‚Äôs salvation, we completely lack any spiritual good before God and as a result, nothing we do can please him (Rom 7:18; Titus 1:15). Our intellects, our emotions, our desires are all subconsciously (and many times consciously) driven towards serving ourselves and not God (Jer 17:9. As we worship ourselves instead of God and our motives in all we do align with that self-worship, it causes all that we do to be displeasing before God who is worthy of our entire worship and every part of our lives (Eph 4:18). Even when we actually do good (which we are still capable of even with darkened understanding), it does not please God because we have not done it for Him, but we have done it for ourselves (Rom 8:8; Ps 14:3). This is where the term ‚Äútotal depravity‚Äù comes from. This term does not mean there is a total inability to do good, but instead, it means that there is nothing outside of Christ that we can do to please God because of this nature we have inherited which has corrupted God‚Äôs original design for us (Isa 64:6; see also Rom 3:9-20).

It is only with Christ and through faith that we are able to please God (John 15:5; Heb 11:6). And because coming to faith and putting our trust in God is something that we are unable to do ourselves because of our natural state of sin, we need God‚Äôs intervention in our lives to bring us to repentance and saving faith (John 6:44).

What effect does our ability to obey God have on our responsibility before God? ¬ß

In the New Testament, Paul has an extensive argument in Romans 1:18-3:20, showing that all people, both Jews and Greeks, stand guilty before God. According to these passages of Scripture, he was convinced that no one was righteous outside of Christ and that every person has sinned and fallen short of God‚Äôs glory. Even John, the beloved disciple, who was especially close to Jesus, affirms that we all have sin in us and we all need to be cleansed from the unrighteousness in us (1 John 1:8-10).

Some have believed and have taught (and still do) that God holds man responsible only for those things that man is able to do. Since God warns us to do good, therefore, we must have the ability to do the good that God commands. This position rejects the doctrine of ‚Äúinherited sin‚Äù (or ‚Äúoriginal sin‚Äù) and maintains that sin consists only in separate sinful acts.

However, the idea that we are responsible before God only for what we are able to do is contrary to the testimony of Scripture, which affirms both that we ‚Äúwere dead in the trespasses and sins‚Äù in which we once walked (Eph 2:1) and thus unable to do any spiritual good, and also that we are all guilty before God. Moreover, if our responsibility before God were limited by our ability, then extremely hardened sinners, who are in great bondage to sin, could be less guilty before God than mature Christians who were striving daily to obey him. And Satan himself, who is eternally able to do only evil, would have no guilt at all ‚Äîsurely an incorrect conclusion.

The true measure of our responsibility and guilt is not our own ability to obey God, but rather the absolute perfection of God‚Äôs moral law and his own holiness (which is reflected in that law). ‚ÄúYou therefore must be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect‚Äù (Matt 5:48). This is clearly an impossible standard and may seem quite hopeless when we realize that there are no works we can do to remove this guilt ourselves. This is where the message of the Gospel is so powerful because Christ, who was and is perfect, achieved for us forgiveness of our sins through is act of obedience on the cross so that we may have salvation through faith in Him and His resurrection instead of trying - and failing - to achieve righteousness through works of our own.

When a Christian sins, how does this affect his legal standing before God? What effect does sin have on a Christian? ¬ß

When a Christian sins, their legal standing before God is unchanged. He or she is still forgiven, for ‚Äúthere is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus‚Äù (Rom 8:1) Salvation is not based on our merits but is a free gift of God (Rom 6:23), and Chris‚Äôs death certainly paid for all our sins‚Äî past, present, and future. Christ died ‚Äúfor our sins‚Äù (1 Cor 15:3) without distinction. In theological terms, we still keep our ‚Äújustification.‚Äô Moreover, we are still children of God, and we still retain our membership in God‚Äôs family. In the same epistle in which John says, ‚ÄúIf we say we have no sin, we deceive ourselves, and the truth is not in us‚Äù (1 John 1:8), he also reminds his readers, ‚ÄúBeloved, we are God‚Äôs children now‚Äù (1 John 3:2). The fact that we have sin remaining in our lives does not mean that we lose our status as God‚Äôs children. In theological terms, we keep our ‚Äúadoption.‚Äù

However, when we sin, even though God does not cease to love us, he is displeased with us. Even among human beings, it is possible to love someone and be displeased with that person at the same time, as any parent, wife, or husband will attest. We see in Scripture that our sin can still grieve God (Eph 4:30) and that, because of God‚Äôs love for us, we may receive discipline from God because of our sin (Heb 12:6-10; Prov 3:11-12). When we disobey, God the Father is grieved, much as an earthly father is grieved with his children‚Äôs disobedience, and he disciplines us out of love (Rev 3:19).

So our sinning may not effect our legal standing before God, but it does disrupt our fellowship with God and it does do damage to our Christian life. The Westminster Confession of Faith wisely says, concerning Christians:

Westminster Confession of Faith (Chapter 11, Section 5)

Although they never can fall from the state of justification, yet they may, by their sins, fall under their God‚Äôs fatherly displeasure, and not have the light of His countenance restored unto them, until they humble themselves, confess their sins, beg pardon, and renew their faith and repentance.

When we sin as Christians, it is not only our personal relationship with God that is disrupted. Our Christian life and fruitfulness in ministry are also damaged (John 15:4). When we stray away from fellowship with Christ because of sin in our lives, we diminish the degree to which we are abiding in Christ. We are warned numerous times throughout the New Testament about the destructive consequences of sin and in many sections of the Epistles, believers are rebuked and discouraged from sin.

Moreover, when we sin as Christians, we suffer a loss of heavenly reward. A person who has built on the work of the church not with gold, silver, and precious stones, but with ‚Äúwood, hay, straw‚Äù (1 Cor 3:12) will have his work ‚Äúburned up‚Äù on the day of judgment and ‚Äúhe will suffer loss, though he himself will be saved, but only as through fire‚Äù (1 Cor 3:15). Paul realizes that ‚Äúwe must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ, so that each one may receive what is due for what he has done in the body‚Äù (2 Cor 5:10). Paul implies that there are degrees of reward in heaven and that sin has negative consequences in terms of loss of heavenly reward.

What is the primary reason that God punishes sin? ¬ß

Although God‚Äôs punishment of sin does serve as a deterrent against further sinning and as a warning to those who observe it, this is not the primary reason why God punishes sin. The primary reason is that God‚Äôs righteousness demands it, so that he might be glorified in the universe that he has created. He is the Lord who practices ‚Äústeadfast love, justice, and righteousness in the earth. For in these things I delight, declares the LORD‚Äù (Jer 9:24).

A God who does not punish sins is not a righteous God. It is like a judge who allows the guilty to stand trial and then receive no punishment for their crimes and walk away freely. Our innate sense of justice within us would cry out against this and our hearts would burn to see justice delivered - so how much more should we hunger and thirst for a righteous God who executes justice perfectly with ultimate fairness? Therefore, when God sent Christ to die and pay the penalty for our sins, He showed how He could still be righteous - He had stored up the punishment due to previous sins (those of Old Testament saints) and then, in perfect righteousness, gave that penalty to Jesus on the cross.

Therefore, in the cross we have a clear demonstration of the reason God punishes sin: If He did not punish sin, He would not be a righteous God, and there would be no ultimate justice in the universe (Rom 3:25-26). But when sin is punished, God is showing himself to be a righteous judge over all, and justice is being done in his universe.
