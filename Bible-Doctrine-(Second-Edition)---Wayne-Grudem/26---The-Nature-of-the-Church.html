The first mark of a true church is right preaching of the Word. Certainly, if the Word of God is not being preached, but simply false doctrines or doctrines of men, then there is no true church. In some cases, we might have difficulty determining just how much wrong doctrine can be tolerated before a church can no longer be considered a true church, but there are many clear cases where we can say that a true church does not exist (for example, the Mormon Church or Jehovah‚Äôs Witnesses). When the preaching of a church conceals from its members the gospel message of salvation by faith alone so that the gospel message is not clearly proclaimed and has not been proclaimed for some time, the group meeting there is not really a church.

The second mark of a true church is the right administration of the sacraments (baptism and the Lord‚Äôs Supper). The traditional Roman Catholic view of the sacraments is that saving grace comes through them which means they are ultimately works by which we earn merit for salvation. In this way, the Roman Catholic Church has insisted on payment rather than teaching faith alone as the means of obtaining salvation, thus obscuring the true gospel. The need to protect the purity of the gospel is one reason for viewing right use of the sacraments as a mark or a true church. Any organisation that begins to practice baptism and the Lord‚Äôs Supper is certainly attempting to function as a church. These sacraments also serve as membership controls for the church. Baptism is the means for admitting people into the church, and the Lord‚Äôs Supper is the means for allowing people to give a sign of continuing in the membership of the church. The church signifies that it considers those who receive baptism and the Lord‚Äôs Supper to be saved, therefore, these activities indicate what a church thinks about salvation, and they are appropriately listed as a mark of a true church. By contrast, groups who do not administer baptism and the Lord‚Äôs Supper signify that they are not intending to function as a church.
