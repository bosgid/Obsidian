Give three evidences from Scripture for the humanity of Christ. ¬ß

  • The Virgin Birth

The birth of Jesus by a human mother is well recorded in the Gospels (Matt 1:18-25; Luke 1:26-35, Luke 2:6-7). The fact that Jesus entered this world through a natural birth is extremely strong evidence of Christ‚Äôs humanity. It also made it possible for the unification of full humanity and full deity in one person since there was no human father involved, only the Holy Spirit.

If Jesus were to have descended from Heaven and not be born of a woman then it would have been difficult to affirm His full humanity for He would not have entered the world as all humans do. And if He was not born of the Spirit then it would have been difficult to affirm His full deity because His conception would have been no different to any other human. Also, if He were to have been conceived by an earthly father, He would have been subject to the curse of Adam. By being born of the Spirit, He was free of Adam‚Äôs inherited nature and guilt (see Chapter 13) so that He may be pure, spotless, and without sin.

  • Jesus had a human body

The fact that Jesus had a human body just like ours is affirmed throughout the Gospels. He was born as a human baby (Luke 2:7); He grew from childhood to adulthood as a normal human would - physically and mentally (Luke 2:40, Luke 2:52); He also experienced tiredness, thirst, and hunger as we all do (John 4:6, John 19:28, Matt 4:2); He experienced physical death on a cross which was the culmination of His human body‚Äôs physical limitations (Luke 23:46).

Jesus also rose from the dead in a physical, human body, though one that was made perfect and was no longer subject to weakness, disease, or death. He demonstrates repeatedly to his disciples that he does have a real physical body (Luke 24:39). Another evidence of this fact is that ‚Äúthey gave him a piece of broiled fish, and he took it and ate before them‚Äù (Luke 24:42-43; cf. v. 30; see also John 20:17, 20, 27; 21:9, 13). In this same human body (though a resurrection body that was made perfect), Jesus also ascended into heaven. (John 16:28; cf. 17:11).

  • Jesus had a human mind, soul, and human emotions

The fact that Jesus ‚Äúincreased in wisdom‚Äù (Luke 2:52) says that he went through a learning process just as children normally do. He learned how to eat, how to talk, how to read and write, and how to be obedient to his parents (see Heb 5:8). This ordinary learning process was part of the genuine humanity of Christ.

We also see several indications that Jesus had a human soul (or spirit). Jesus mentions His soul/spirit a few times around the events of the crucifixion (John 12:27; John 13:21; Matt 26:38).

Jesus also had a full range of human emotions (Matt 8:10; John 11:35; Mark 3:5; Matt 14:14; Luke 22:44; Luke 10:21; Heb 5:7).

Why was Jesus‚Äô deity necessary? ¬ß

It is crucially important to affirm the full deity of Christ as it is clearly taught in Scripture. It is important because only someone who is infinite God could bear the full penalty for all the sins of all those who would believe in him. Any finite creature would have been incapable of bearing that penalty. Also, salvation is from the Lord (Jonah 2:9), and the whole message of Scripture is designed to show that no human being, no creature, could ever save man - only God himself. Along with that, Christ‚Äôs deity is important because only someone who was truly and fully God could be the one mediator between God and man (1 Tim 2:5), both to bring us back to God and also to reveal God most fully to us (John 14:9). Thus, if Jesus is not fully God, we have no salvation and ultimately no Christianity.

In what ways were the following views erroneous in their view of Christ? ¬ß

  • Arianism

The term Arianism is derived from Arius, a bishop of Alexandria, who taught that God the Son was at one point created by God the Father, and that before that time the Son did not exist, nor did the Holy Spirit, but the Father only. Arianism teaches that though the Son is a heavenly being who existed before the rest of creation and who is far greater than all the rest of creation, he is still not equal to the Father in all his attributes - he may even be said to be ‚Äúlike the Father‚Äù or ‚Äúsimilar to the Father‚Äù in his nature, but he cannot be said to be ‚Äúof the same nature‚Äù as the Father.

For one to hold the view of Arianism they must then deny the full deity of Christ - which clearly goes against what is affirmed by Scripture. This view describes Christ more as someone who is fully man and fully angelic instead of fully man and fully God.

  • Apollinarianism

Apollinaris, who became bishop in Laodicea about AD 361, taught that the one person of Christ had a human body but not a human mind or spirit, and that the mind and spirit of Christ were from the divine nature of the Son of God.

The views of Apollinaris were rejected by the leaders of the church at that time, who realized that it was not just our human body that needed salvation and needed to be represented by Christ in his redemptive work, but our human minds and spirits (or souls) as well. Christ had to be fully and truly man if he was to save us (Heb 2:17) and not one part man and another part God.

  • Nestorianism

Nestorianism is the doctrine that there were two separate persons in Christ, a human person and a divine person, a teaching that is distinct from the biblical view that sees Jesus as one person. This belief originated from a man named Nestorius who was a popular preacher at Antioch, and from AD 428 was bishop of Constantinople.

The church at the time rejected this view of Christ because nowhere in Scripture do we have an indication chat the human nature of Christ, for example, is an independent person, deciding to do something contrary to the divine nature of Christ. Nowhere do we have an indication of the human and divine natures talking to each other or struggling within Christ, or any such thing. Rather, we have a consistent picture of a single person acting in wholeness and unity. Jesus always speaks as ‚ÄúI,‚Äù not as ‚Äúwe,‚Äù though he can refer to himself and the Father together as ‚Äúwe‚Äù (John 14:23). The Bible always speaks of Jesus as ‚Äúhe,‚Äù not as ‚Äúthey.‚Äù And, though we can sometimes distinguish actions of his divine nature and actions of his human nature in order to help us understand some of the statements and actions recorded in Scripture, the Bible itself never speaks of separate persons, but always talks about what the person of Christ did.

  • Monophysitismis

Eutyches, who was the leader of a monastery at Constantinople, taught the opposite error from Nestorianism, for he denied that the human nature and divine nature in Christ remained fully human and fully divine. His view of monophysitism was that Christ had one nature only (Gk. monos, ‚Äúone,‚Äù and physis, ‚Äúnature‚Äù). He believed that the human nature of Christ was taken up and absorbed into the divine nature, so that both natures were changed somewhat, and a third kind of nature resulted - like a drop of ink mixing with a glass of water to create a new mixture which is neither pure ink nor pure water.

Monophysitism also rightly caused great concern in the church, because, by this doctrine, Christ was neither truly God nor truly man. And if that was so, he could not truly represent us as a man nor could he be true God and able to earn our salvation.

The Chalcedonian Definition

We, then, following the holy Fathers, all with one consent, teach men to confess one and the same Son, our Lord Jesus Christ, the same perfect in Godhead and also perfect in manhood; truly God and truly man, of a reasonable [rational] soul and body; consubstantial [coessential] with the Father according to the Godhead, and consubstantial with us according to the Manhood; in all things like unto us, without sin; begotten before all ages of the Father according to the Godhead, and in these latter days, for us and for our salvation, born of the Virgin Mary, the Mother of God, according to the Manhood; one and the same Christ, Son, Lord, Only-begotten, to be acknowledged in two natures, inconfusedly, unchangeably, indivisibly, inseparably; the distinction of natures being by no means taken away by the union, but rather the property of each nature being preserved, and concurring in one Person and one Subsistence, not parted or divided into two persons, but one and the same Son, and only begotten, God, the Word, the Lord Jesus Christ, as the prophets from the beginning [have declared] concerning him, and the Lord Jesus Christ himself has taught us, and the Creed of the holy Fathers has been handed down to us.

Against the view of Apollinaris that Christ did not have a human mind or soul, we have the statement that he was ‚Äùtruly man, of a reasonable soul and body‚Ä¶ consubstantial with us according to the Manhood; in all things like unto us.‚Äù

In opposition to the view of Nestorianism that Christ was two persons united in one body, we have the words ‚Äùindivisibly, inseparably‚Ä¶ concurring in one Person and one Subsistence, not parted or divided into two persons.‚Äù

Against the view of Eutychianism that Christ had only one nature and that his human nature was lost in the union with the divine nature, we have the words ‚Äúto be acknowledged in two natures, inconfusedly, unchangeably‚Ä¶ the distinction of natures being by no means taken away by the union, but rather the property of each nature being preserved.‚Äù The human and the divine natures were not confused or changed when Christ became man, but the human nature remained a truly human nature, and the divine nature remained a truly divine nature.

If we are willing to affirm the Chalcedonian statement about ‚Äùthe property of each nature being preserved‚Äù in the person of Christ, it is necessary to distinguish between things done by Chris‚Äôs human nature but not his divine nature, or by his divine nature but not his human nature. For example, when we are talking about Jesus‚Äô human nature, we can say that he ascended to heaven and is no longer in the world (John 16:28; 17:11; Acts 1:9-11). So we can say that both things are true about the person of Christ‚Äîhe has returned to heaven and he is also present with us.

Similarly, we can say that Jesus was about thirty years old (Luke 3:23), if we are speaking with respect to his human nature, but we can say that he eternally existed (John 1:1-2; John 8:58) if we are speaking of his divine nature.

In his human nature, Jesus was weak and tired (Matt 4:2; 8:24; Mark 15:21; John 4:6), but in his divine nature he was omnipotent (Matt 8:26-27; Col 1:17; Heb 1:3). Particularly striking is the scene of the Sea of Galilee where Jesus was asleep on the stern of the boat, presumably because he was weary (Matt 8:24). But he was able to arise from his sleep and calm the wind and sea with a word (Matt 8:26-27).

In a similar way, we can understand that in his human nature, Jesus died (Luke 23:46; 1 Cor 15:3). But with respect to his divine nature, he did not die, but was able to raise himself from the dead (John 2:19; John 10:17-18; Heb 7:16). And it is not correct to say that Jesus‚Äô divine nature died, or could die, if ‚Äúdie‚Äù means a cessation of activity, a cessation of consciousness, or a diminution of power. Nevertheless, by virtue of union with Jesus‚Äô human nature, his divine nature somehow tasted something of what it was like to go through death. The person of Christ experienced death.

Regardless of what it is that Christ does, it is still the whole person of Christ that does it and experiences it. It is never just a part of him even when he is operating according to a specific nature. Whatever can be said of one nature or the other can be said of the person of Christ.
