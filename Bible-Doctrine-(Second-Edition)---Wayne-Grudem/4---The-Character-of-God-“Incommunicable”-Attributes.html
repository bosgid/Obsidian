The communicable attributes of God: The attributes that God shares or ‚Äúcommunicates‚Äù with us. (i.e. Attributes man can also have - but not at the same level as God has them) Examples: His love, His mercy, His justice

Define God‚Äôs ‚Äúindependence.‚Äù How can the two parts of this definition be reconciled? ¬ß

God‚Äôs Independence

God does not need us or the rest of creation for anything, yet we and the rest of creation glorify him and bring him joy.

(a.k.a. God‚Äôs self-existence or aseity)

Scripture in several places teaches that God does not need any part of creation in order to exist or for any other reason. God is absolutely independent and self-sufficient. Paul proclaims to the men of Athens, ‚ÄùThe God who made the world and everything in it, being Lord of heaven and earth, does not live in temples made by man, nor is he served by human hands, as though he needed anything, since he himself gives to all mankind life and breath and everything‚Äù (Acts 17:24-25). The implication is that God does not need anything from mankind (see also Job 41:11; Ps 50:10-12). Not even our fellowship out of loneliness as this would mean that He is not independent of His creation. He has no needs that creation can fulfill. God‚Äôs being is also something totally unique. It is not just that God does not need the creation for anything; God could not need the creation for anything. The difference between the creature and the Creator is an immensely vast difference, for God exists in a fundamentally different order of being. No limitation or imperfection in creation should be projected on to our thought of God. He is the Creator; all else is creaturely. All else can pass away in an instant; he necessarily exists forever.

However, God‚Äôs independence does not make His creation (and us) meaningless. We and the rest of creation do in fact glorify God, and we do bring him joy. God has created us and has determined that we would be meaningful to Him. That is the final definition of genuine significance. God speaks of his sons and daughters from the ends of the earth as ‚Äùeveryone who is called by my name, whom I created for my glory, whom I formed and made‚Äù (Isa 43:7). Although God did not have to create us, He chose to do so in a totally free choice. He decided that He would create us to glorify Him (cf. Eph 1:11-12; Rev 4:11). It is also true that we are able to bring real joy and delight to God. It is one of the most amazing facts in Scripture that God actually delights in his people and rejoices over them. Zephaniah prophesies that the Lord ‚Äùwill rejoice over you with gladness; he will quiet you by his love; he will exult over you with loud singing‚Äù (Zeph 3:17; cf. Isa 62:3-5). God does not need us for anything, yet it is the amazing fact of our existence that He chooses to delight in us and to allow us to bring joy to His heart. This is the basis for personal significance in the lives of all God‚Äôs people: to be significant to God is to be significant in the most ultimate sense. No greater personal significance can be imagined.

In light of God‚Äôs unchangeableness, what does Scripture mean when it speaks of God changing his mind? ¬ß

God‚Äôs Unchangeableness

God is unchanging in his being, perfections, purposes, and promises, yet God does act and feel emotions, and he acts and feels differently in response to different situations.

(a.k.a. God‚Äôs immutability)

Examples of God ‚Äúchanging His mind‚Äù include the successful intervention of Moses in prayer to prevent the destruction of the people of Israel (Exod 32:9-14), the adding of another fifteen years to the life of Hezekiah (Isa 38:1-6), or the failure to bring promised judgment upon Nineveh when the people repented (Jonah 3:4, 10).

These instances should all be understood as true expressions of God‚Äôs present attitude or intention with respect to the situation as it exists at that moment. If the situation changes, then of course God‚Äôs attitude or expression of intention will also change. This is just saying that God responds differently to different situations. This differs to God‚Äôs overarching plan and purpose over a situation. That will never change. But as the events play out during the completion of a plan or purpose, God‚Äôs response and attitude toward us will also vary according to each event. It would not make sense to us if God were to have a single attitude or response to every situation based purely on its outcome. God can allow something to happen to us for our own good, but still draw close and comfort during the trial and then rejoice with us at the fruit produced from that same trial. How strange would it be if God were to only rejoice through all the events of that trial in our life?

As in many other places in Scripture, God responded to the new situation differently, but nevertheless remained unchanging in His being and His purposes. In fact, if God did not respond differently when people acted differently, then people‚Äôs actions would make no difference to God, and He would not be the kind of just and merciful God portrayed in the Bible - unchanging in His attributes of justice and mercy.

Does time have any effect on God? Explain. ¬ß

God‚Äôs Eternity

God has no beginning, end, or succession of moments in his own being, and he sees all time equally vividly, yet God sees events in time and acts in time.

(a.k.a. God‚Äôs infinity with respect to time)

God created time and is therefore outside of, and beyond, time. Reference verses: Psalm 90:2, Job 36:26, Revelation 1:8, Jude 1:25

He also sees all of time equally and vividly, remembering all detailed events over thousands of years as clearly as one would remember the events of yesterday while also experiencing a single day in so much detail that it would seem like a thousand years has passed through that single day (Ps 90:4, 2 Pet 3:8).

So time has no effect on Him, but He does have an effect on it and works within it. We must therefore affirm both that God has no succession of moments in his own being and sees all history equally vividly, and that in his creation he sees the progress of events over time and acts differently at different points in time. In short, he is the Lord who created time and who rules over it and uses it for his own purposes. God can act in time because he is Lord of time.

If God is present everywhere, how can he be present in hell if it is a place of terrible suffering? ¬ß

God‚Äôs Omnipresence

God does not have size or spatial dimensions and is present at every point of space with his whole being, yet God acts differently in different places.

God is Lord over all space because He has created it (Deut 10:14). He is present everywhere (Jer 23:23-24), and not just a part of Him split up into separate spaces, but His entire being is present everywhere always. There is nowhere in the entire universe that once can hide from God - not even in hell (a.k.a. Sheol in the Old Testament).

Psalm 139:7-10

  1. Where shall I go from your Spirit? Or where shall I flee from your presence?

  2. If I ascend to heaven, you are there! If I make my bed in Sheol, you are there!

  3. If I take the wings of the morning and dwell in the uttermost parts of the sea,

  4. even there your hand shall lead me, and your right hand shall hold me.

The analogy of a sponge filled with water is not perfect, but it is helpful. Water is present everywhere in the sponge, but the water is still completely distinct from the sponge. Now this analogy breaks down at very small points within the sponge, where we could say that there is sponge at one point and not water, or water and not sponge. Yet this is because the analogy is dealing with two materials that have spatial characteristics and dimensions, while God does not.

We must also realize that God is present in different ways in different places. Another way of understanding this is to say that God acts differently in different places in his creation. Sometimes God is present to punish, and it seems that this is how God is present in hell. A terrifying passage in Amos vividly portrays this presence of God in judgment:

This also applies to His blessing. He can be present for the purpose of blessing and this is shown in His manifest presence in the temple, the arch of the covenant, or even amidst believers gathered in Christ‚Äôs name.

The Unity of God

God is not divided into parts, yet we see different attributes of God emphasized at different times.

When Scripture speaks about God‚Äôs attributes, it never singles out one attribute of God as more important than all the rest. There is an assumption that every attribute is completely true of God and is true of all of God‚Äôs character. For example, John can say that ‚ÄùGod is light‚Äù (1 John 1:5) and then a little later say also that ‚ÄùGod is love‚Äù (1 John 4:8). There is no suggestion that part of God is light and part of God is love, or that God is partly light and partly love. Nor should we think that God is more light than love, or more love than light. Rather, it is God himself who is light, and it is God himself who is also love. God‚Äôs whole being includes all of his attributes: He is entirely loving, entirely merciful, entirely just, and so forth. Every attribute of God that we find in Scripture is true of all of God‚Äôs being, and we therefore can say that every attribute of God also qualifies every other attribute.

These attributes of God are spoken about separately in the Bible for our own sake since we cannot grasp the full unity of God with our limited understanding. We must be aware of this so to avoid thinking, for example, that God is a loving God at one point in history and a just or wrathful God at another point in history. He is the same God always, and everything He says or does is fully consistent with all His attributes. It is not accurate to say, as some have said, that God is a God of justice in the Old Testament and a God of love in the New Testament. God is and always has been infinitely just and infinitely loving as well, and everything He does in the Old Testament as well as the New Testament is completely consistent with both of those attributes. Different parts of the Bible may demonstrate or highlight specific attributes more than others at times, but we should not focus on these parts of Scripture and ignore the rest of the Bible by choosing to single out and emphasise certain attributes over others.
