10 - Angels, Satan, and Demons

Feb 05, 2024, 16 min read

What are angels? ¬ß

Angels

Angels are created spiritual beings with moral judgment and high intelligence but without physical bodies.

Angels have not always existed; they are part of the universe that God created (Neh 9:6; cf. Ps 148:2, 5). Paul tells us that God created all things ‚Äúvisible and invisible‚Äù through Christ and for him, and then specifically includes the angelic world with the phrase ‚Äúwhether thrones or dominions or rulers or authorities‚Äù (Col 1:16). Angels exercise moral judgment seeing as some of them sinned and fell from their positions (2 Pet 2:4; Jude 1:6). Their high intelligence is seen throughout Scripture as they speak to people (Matt 28:5; Acts 12:6-11) and sing praise to God (Rev 4:11; 5:11-12). Since angels are ‚Äúspirits‚Äù (Heb 1:14), or spiritual creatures, they do not ordinarily have physical bodies (Luke 24:39). Therefore, they cannot usually be seen by us unless God gives us a special ability to see them (Num 22:31; 2 Kgs 6:17; Luke 2:13). In their ordinary activities of guarding and protecting us (Ps 34:7; 91:11; Heb 1:14) and joining with us in worship to God (Heb 12:22), they are invisible. However, from time to time angels took on a bodily form to appear to various people in Scripture (Matt 28:5; Heb 13:2).

Scripture sometimes uses other terms for angels, such as ‚Äúsons of God‚Äù (Job 1:6; 2:1), ‚Äúholy ones‚Äù (Ps 89:5, 7), ‚Äùministering spirits‚Äù (Heb 1:14), ‚Äúwatchers‚Äù (Dan 4:13, 17, 2323), ‚Äúthrones,‚Äù ‚Äúdominions,‚Äù ‚Äúrulers,‚Äù ‚Äúauthorities‚Äù (Col 1:16), and ‚Äúpower‚Äù (Eph 1:21). There are three other specific types of heavenly beings named in Scripture. Whether we think of these as special types of angels (in a broad sense of the term), or whether we think of them as heavenly beings distinct from angels, they are nonetheless created spiritual beings who serve and worship God. These are the ‚Äúcherubim‚Äù (Gen 3:24; Ps 18:10; Ezek 10:1-22; Exod 25:18-22), the ‚Äúseraphim‚Äù (Isa 6:2-7), and the ‚Äúliving creatures‚Äù (Ezek 1:5-14; Rev 4:6-8).

Name three purposes for angels ¬ß

  • Angels show the greatness of God‚Äôs love and plan for us.

Human beings and angels are the only moral, highly intelligent creatures that God has made. Therefore, we can understand much about God‚Äôs plan and love for us when we compare ourselves with angels. The first distinction to be noted is that angels are never said to be made ‚Äúin the image of God,‚Äù while human beings are several times said to be in Gods image (Gen 1:26-27; 9:6). Since being in the image of God means to be like God, it seems fair to conclude that we are more like God even than the angels are. This is supported by the fact that God will someday give us authority over angels to judge them (1 Cor 6:3). When our salvation is complete, we will be exalted above angels and rule over them. In fact, even now, angels already serve us (Heb 1:14). The ability of human beings to bear children like themselves is another element of our superiority to angels, who apparently cannot bear children (cf. Matt 22:30; Luke 20:34-36). Angels also demonstrate the greatness of God‚Äôs love for us in that, though many angels sinned, none were saved (2 Pet 2:4; Jude 1:6; Heb 2:16).

We see, therefore, that God created two groups of intelligent moral creatures. Among the angels, many sinned, but God decided to redeem none of them. This was perfectly just for God to do, and no angel can ever complain that he has been treated unfairly by God. However, even though all human beings have sinned and turned away from him, God decided to do much more than merely meet the demands of justice; he decided to save some sinful human beings. In fact, he has decided to redeem out of sinful mankind a great multitude, whom no man can number. This is incalculable mercy and love, far beyond our comprehension. It is all undeserved favour; it is all of grace. The striking contrast with the fate of angels brings this truth home to us.

  • Angels remind us that the unseen world is real.

Many in our day deny the reality of anything they cannot see. But the biblical teaching on the existence of angels is a constant reminder to us that there is an unseen world that is very real. It was only when the Lord opened the eyes of Elisha‚Äôs servant to the reality of this invisible world that the servant saw that ‚Äúthe mountain was full of horses and chariots of fire all around Elisha‚Äù (2 Kings 6:17). The psalmist, too, shows an awareness of the unseen world when he encourages the angels, ‚ÄúPraise him, all his angels; praise him, all his hosts!‚Äù (Ps 148:2). The author of Hebrews reminds us that when we worship, we come into the heavenly Jerusalem to gather with ‚Äúinnumerable angels in festal gathering‚Äù (Heb 12:22), whom we do not see, but whose presence should fill us with both awe and joy. An unbelieving world may dismiss talk of angels as mere superstition, but Scripture offers these statements as insight into the state of affairs as they really are.

  • Angels carry out some of God‚Äôs plans.

Scripture sees angels as God‚Äôs servants who carry out some of his plans in the earth. They bring God‚Äôs messages to people (Luke 1:11-19; Acts 8:26; 10:3-8, 22; 27:23-24). They carry out some of God‚Äôs judgements (2 Sam 24:16-17; 2 Chr 32:21; Acts 12:23; Rev 16:1). When Christ returns, angels will come with him as a great army accompanying their King and Lord (Matt 16:27; Luke 9:26; 2 Thess 1:7). Angels also patrol the earth as God‚Äôs representatives (Zech 1:10-11) and carry out war against demonic forces (Dan 10:13; Rev 12:7-8, 20:1-3). When Christ returns, an archangel will proclaim his coming (1 Thess 4:16; cf. Rev 18:1-2, 21; 19:17-18; etc.).

What should be our relationship to angels? ¬ß

Healthy relationship toward angels:

Scripture makes it clear that God wants us to be aware of the existence of angels and of the nature of their activity. There are several ways in which our Christian lives will be enriched by an awareness of the existence and ministry of angels in the world even today.

When we come before God in worship, we are joining a great multitude of angels (Heb 12:22-23). Though we do not ordinarily see or hear evidence of this heavenly worship, it certainly enriches our sense of reverence and joy in God‚Äôs presence if we appreciate the fact that angels join us in the worship of God.

Moreover, we should be aware that angels are watching our obedience or disobedience to God through the day. There are perhaps hundreds of angels who are ‚Äúlonging to look‚Äù (cf. 1 Pet 1:12) at the way Christ‚Äôs great salvation finds expression in our lives. This can sober us in our thoughts towards acts of disobedience or this can give us great encouragement for the ‚Äúunseen‚Äù moments of obedience through difficult times. As if to make the reality of angelic observation of our service to God more vivid, the author of Hebrews suggests that angels can sometimes take human form, apparently to make ‚Äúinspection visits‚Äù (Heb 13:2; cf. Gen 18:2-5; 19:1-3). This should make us eager to minister to the needs of others whom we do not know, all the while wondering if someday we will reach heaven and meet the angel whom we helped when he appeared temporarily as a human being in distress here on earth.

When we are suddenly delivered from a danger or distress, we might suspect that angels have been sent by God to help us, and we should be thankful. There are many examples in the Bible of this happening (Dan 6:22; Acts 5:19-20; 12:7-11; Matt 4:11; Ps 91:11-12).

Unhealthy relationship towards angels:

The Bible warns against receiving false doctrine from supposed angels (Gal 1:8; 2 Cor 11:14; 1 Kgs 13:18). If any supposed angel where to reveal anything contrary to the teachings of Scripture then we as Christians should deny those claims and treat the angel as a deceiver so that we are not fooled into evil.

We should not worship angels, pray to them, or seek them. ‚ÄúWorship of angels‚Äù (Col 2:18) was one of the false doctrines being taught at Colossa. Moreover, an angel speaking to John in the book of Revelation warns John not to worship him (Rev 19:10). Nor should we pray to angels. We are to pray to God alone, who alone is omnipotent and thus able to answer prayer and who alone is omniscient and therefore able to hear the prayers of all his people at once. Paul warns us against thinking that any other ‚Äúmediator‚Äù can come between us and God (1 Tim 2:5). If we were to pray to angels, it would be implicitly attributing to them a status equal to God, which we must not do. There is no example in Scripture of anyone praying to any specific angel or asking angels for help.

Moreover, Scripture gives us no warrant to seek for appearances of angels to us. They manifest themselves unsought. To seek such appearances would seem to indicate an unhealthy curiosity or a desire for some kind of spectacular event rather than a love for God and devotion to him and his work. Though angels did appear to people at various times in Scripture, the people apparently never sought those appearances. Our role is rather to talk to the Lord, who is himself the commander of all angelic forces. However, it would not seem wrong to ask God to fulfill his promise in Psalm 91:11 to send angels to protect us in times of need.

How did demons originate? ¬ß

Demons

Demons are evil angels who sinned against God and who now continually work evil in the world.

When God created the world, called it good (Gen 1:31). This means that even the angelic world that God had created did not have evil angels or demons in it at that time. But by the time of Genesis 3, we find that Satan, in the form of a serpent, is tempting Eve to sin (Gen 3:1-5). Therefore, sometime between the events of Genesis 1:31 and Genesis 3:1, there must have been a rebellion in the angelic world with many angels turning against God and becoming evil. The New Testament speaks of this in two places: 2 Peter 2:4 and Jude 1:6. Note that the emphasis is on the fact that they are removed from the glory of God‚Äôs presence and their activity is restricted, but the text does not imply either that the influence of demons has been removed from the world or that some demons are kept in a place of punishment apart from the world while others are able to influence it. Rather, both 2 Peter and Jude tell us that some angels rebelled against God and became hostile opponents to his word. Their sin seems to have been pride and a refusal to accept their assigned place.

Can Christians be influenced by demons? Explain. ¬ß

Satan and demons will use whatever means necessary to influence a Christian into sin. This influence is something we are called to stand strong against as believers (James 4:7; Eph 6:11-13), therefore, there is clearly ways that the devil and and demons can affect us if we allow it. But even through any influence or failure to resist, we have an assurance through the cross that we are still kept safe by the blood of Christ (1 John 5:18).

As Christians, we cannot be unaware that the world we live in is under the power of sin and the devil (Eph 2:2; John 12:31, 14:30, 16:11; Luke 4:5-6). This is the current state of the world until Christ‚Äôs return, even though the power of the enemy is limited and not beyond God‚Äôs sovereign control. This means that the devil will use the world against us to tempt us into sin. Sinning, even by Christians, does open more opportunities for the devil to work and influence our lives (Eph 4:26-27) and any practice of sinning is from the devil (1 John 3:8-10). Not all sin is caused by Satan or demons, nor is the major influence or cause of sin demonic activity, but demonic activity is probably a factor in almost all sin and almost all destructive activity that opposes the work of God in the world today. Our sinful nature and our flesh is enough to cause us to sin, but all the temptations and evil in this world - hidden or revealed - is like gasoline on the flame of sin burning within all of us.

There is even a measure of influence that the devil and demons can have on our churches and in our congregations. This comes in the form of false teachings which, according to the New Testament, can originate from demons (1 Tim 4:1, 2 Tim 2:24-26). This is one of the worst ways in which the enemy can influence a Christian, for the truth of God is taken, twisted slightly, and used against the very children the words were written for. The disgust of this practice is on full display by the apostle Peter in 2 Peter 2.

It is important to mention that when we look at the overall emphasis of the New Testament epistles, we realize that very little space is given to discussing demonic activity in the lives of believers or methods to resist and oppose such activity. The emphasis is on telling believers not to sin but to live lives of righteousness. For example, in 1 Corinthians, when there is a problem of ‚Äúdivisions,‚Äù Paul does not tell the church to rebuke a spirit of dissension, but simply urges them to ‚Äúagree‚Äù and ‚Äúbe united in the same mind and the same judgment‚Äù (1 Cor 1:10). When there is a problem of incest, he does not tell the Corinthians to rebuke a spirit of incest, but tells them that they ought to be outraged and that they should exercise church discipline until the offender repents (1 Cor 5:1-5). These examples could be duplicated many times in the other New Testament epistles. Therefore, though the New Testament clearly recognizes the influence of demonic activity in the world, and even, as we shall see, upon the lives of believers, its primary focus regarding Christian growth is not on demonic activity, but on the choices and actions taken by Christians themselves (see also Gal 5:16-26; Eph 4:1-6:9; Col 3:1-4:6; etc.). Similarly, this should be the primary focus of our efforts today when we strive to grow in holiness and faith and to overcome the sinful desires and actions that remain in our lives (cf. Rom 6:1-23) and to overcome the temptations that come against us from an unbelieving world (1 Cor 10:13). We need to accept our own responsibility to obey the Lord and not to shift blame for our own misdeeds onto some demonic force.

In what ways can Christians exert authority over demons? ¬ß

We have been given Christ‚Äôs authority to use on his behalf in our lives. It is not our own authority - it is Christ‚Äôs authority which we use in his name. Imagine a messenger coming with a written command that has the king‚Äôs seal. This written letter will allow the messenger to do anything written on that letter with no restrictions because of the authority of the king given to the messenger. The authority of Christ given to us has power and authority of demons and we should be confident to rebuke any form of demonic influence in the name of Jesus and command it to leave (Luke 9:1, 10:17-19; Acts 8:7, 16:18).

We are also given many other tools to stand firm against the enemy and to exert authority over demons. These are all clearly described by the apostle Paul as the ‚Äúarmour of God‚Äù (Eph 6:10-18).

We as believers also have the authority to resist (James 4:7; 1 Pet 5:8-9). Sin and the fear of death no longer has power over us, therefore, we have full authority to resist any schemes of the enemy (1 John 4:4). It is important that we recognize that the work of Christ on the cross is the ultimate basis for our authority over demons. Christ has defeated death and the enemy through the cross (Heb 2:14; Col 2:15).

We also have the authority of the Gospel to use against the enemy. If we really believe the scriptural testimony to the existence and activity of demons, and if we really believe that ‚Äúthe reason the Son of God appeared was to destroy the works of the devil‚Äù (1 John 3:8), then it would seem appropriate to expect that even today when the gospel is proclaimed to unbelievers and when prayer is made for believers who have perhaps been unaware of this dimension of spiritual conflict, there will be a genuine and often immediately recognizable triumph over the power of the enemy (Luke 4:41; Acts 8:7, Acts 26:16-18; 2 Cor 10:3-4). We should expect that this would happen, think of it as a normal part of the work of Christ in building up his kingdom, and rejoice in Christ‚Äôs victory in it.

With all this in mind, we must always remember that Jesus issues a clear warning that we should not rejoice too much or become proud in our power over demons, but that we should rejoice rather in our great salvation (Luke 10:17-20).
