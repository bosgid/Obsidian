The postmillennial perspective does not view the millennium as a fixed period of a thousand years at some point of time in history. Rather, what is envisioned is more like a ‚Äúmillennial age‚Äù of peace and righteousness that is gradually more prevalent on earth as the progress of the gospel and the growth of the church increases over time so that a larger and larger proportion of the world‚Äôs population will be Christians. While premillennialists talk about a renewed earth with Jesus Christ physically present and reigning as King, together with glorified believers in resurrection bodies, postmillennialists are simply talking about an earth with many, many Christians influencing society. ÔªøÔªøÔªøWhat are the primary distinctions between the classical premillennial view and the dispensational premillennial view? ¬ß

According to both the classical and dispensational premillennial, Christ will return before the millennial. The classical premillennial view holds that Christ will only return once before the millennial after the great tribulation is complete. However, the dispensational premillennial view holds that there are two points in time when Christ returns (instead of just one): once before the great tribulation and then again after the great tribulation is complete.

According to this view, the church age will continue until, suddenly, unexpectedly, and secretly, Christ will return part way to earth, and then will call believers to Himself: ‚ÄúThe dead in Christ will rise first. Then we who are alive, who are left, will be caught up together with them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air‚Äù (1 Thess 4:16-17). Christ will then return to heaven with the believers who have been removed from the earth. When that happens, there will be a great tribulation on the earth for a period of seven years. During this seven-year period of tribulation, many of the signs that were predicted to precede Christ‚Äôs return will be fulfilled (classical premillennial position believe that all these predicted signs would have already occurred before Christ‚Äôs one and only return). During the tribulation period, the great ingathering of the fullness of the Jewish people will occur as they trust Christ as their Messiah. In the midst of great suffering, there will also be much effective evangelism, especially carried out by the new Jewish Christians. At the end of the tribulation, Christ will then come back a second time with his saints to reign on the earth for one thousand years. After this millennial period, there will be a rebellion resulting in the final defeat of Satan and his forces, and then will come the resurrection of unbelievers, the last judgement, and the beginning of the eternal state.

These two views are almost identical regarding the period from Christ‚Äôs return to establish the millennium reign over earth and onwards. However, the period between the Church Age and this point of time is where the major differences occur. This pretribulational view is found almost exclusively among dispensationalists who wish to maintain a clear distinction between the church and Israel. This pretribulational viewpoint allows the distinction to be maintained, since the church is taken out of the world before the widespread conversion of the Jewish people. These Jewish people therefore remain a distinct group from the church. This is quite different to the beliefs of most people with a classical premillennial view who generally do not believe in this distinction between the church and Israel.

  1. Lack of Scriptural Evidence for Pre-Tribulation Rapture

The New Testament nowhere clearly says that the church will be taken out of the world before the tribulation. For example, it is very difficult to understand 1 Thessalonians 4:17, the only passage that explicitly speaks of the fact that the church will be ‚Äúcaught up‚Äù (or ‚Äúraptured‚Äù), to speak of the idea of a secret coming of Christ to take believers out of the world prior to the tribulation.   1. Scriptural Consistency with a Single, Post-Tribulation Trumpet Call

The tribulation is quite clearly linked with the Lord‚Äôs return in some passages. First, the trumpet call to gather the elect in Matthew 24:31, the sound of the trumpet of God in 1 Thessalonians 4:16, and the last trumpet at which our bodies are changed in 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 all seem to be the same trumpet‚Äîthe last trumpet that is blown just before the millennium. If it is indeed the ‚Äúlast trumpet‚Äù (1 Cor 15:52), then it is hard to see how another loud trumpet call (Matt 24:31) could follow it seven years later.

  1. Jesus‚Äôs words are intended for the church - not specifically for the Jews - when addressing His disciples

Matthew 24 is very difficult to understand as referring not to the church but to Jewish people who would be saved during the tribulation. Jesus is addressing His disciples (Matt 24:1-4) and warning them of persecution and suffering to come. It does not seem likely that Jesus, in saying all these things to His disciples, intended His words to apply not to the church but only to a future earthly kingdom of Jewish people who would be converted during the tribulation. Nor does it seem likely that the disciples are representatives of a future Jewish kingdom and not representatives of the church, with whose founding they were so integrally connected as to be its foundation (Eph 2:20).

  1. Absence of Biblical Basis for Two Separate Returns

Finally, the New Testament does not seem to justify the idea of two separate returns of Christ (once for his church before the tribulation and then seven years later with his church to bring judgment on unbelievers). No such view is explicitly taught in any passage, but it is simply an inference drawn from differences between various passages that describe Christ‚Äôs return from different perspectives. But it is not at all difficult to see these passages as referring to a single event occurring at one time.
