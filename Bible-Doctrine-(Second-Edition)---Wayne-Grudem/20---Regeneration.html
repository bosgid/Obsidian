Regeneration

Does a person play any active role in regeneration? Explain. ¬ß

In the work of regeneration, we play no active role at all. It is instead totally a work of God. We see this, for example, when John talks about those to whom Christ gave power to become children of God: they ‚Äúwere born, not of blood nor of the will of the flesh nor of the will of man, but of God‚Äù (John 1:13). Here John specifies that children of God are those who are ‚Äúborn‚Ä¶ of God,‚Äù and our human will (‚Äúthe will of man‚Äù) does not bring about this kind of birth. The fact that we are passive in regeneration is also evident when Scripture refers to it as being ‚Äúborn‚Äù or being ‚Äúborn again‚Äù (see James 1:18; 1 Pet 1:3; John 3:3-8). We played no part in our physical birth, therefore, using the language ‚Äúborn‚Äù and ‚Äúborn again‚Äù makes a clear connection to this work of regeneration being something that we are passively a part of and play no role in. This sovereign work of God in regeneration was also predicted in the prophecy of Ezekiel. Through him, God promised a time in the future when he would be the one to give new spiritual life to his people (Ezek 36:26-27).

Another concept used by Scripture when speaking of regeneration is the image of being brought from death to life (Eph 2:4-6; Col 2:13; Rom 6:4). This also creates a strong link with our inability to save ourselves. A dead person has no life and is completely unable to move, speak, think, or take any action. An external power is required to raise someone from death to life. It is like Jesus calling Lazarus out of the tomb (John 11:38-44). Lazarus was dead and unable to come out of the tomb on his own. It was only by the call of Jesus and the power of God that he was able to come back to life and he played no role in it.

Compare and contrast effective calling and regeneration. ¬ß

Through the descriptions of regeneration, Scripture indicates that regeneration must come before we can respond to effective calling with saving faith. An external power is required to do work within us to enable us to effectively respond to the call of God. Therefore, we can say that regeneration comes before the result of effective calling (our faith). As the gospel comes to us (general calling), God speaks through it to summon us to himself (effective calling) and to give us new spiritual life (regeneration) so that we are enabled to respond in faith. Effective calling is thus God the Father speaking powerfully to us, and regeneration is God the Father and God the Holy Spirit working powerfully in us to make us alive.

What is the relationship between regeneration and saving faith? ¬ß

Regeneration is defined to be the act of God awakening spiritual life within us, bringing us from spiritual death to spiritual life. On this definition, it is natural to understand that regeneration comes before saving faith. It is in fact this work of God that gives us the spiritual ability to respond to God in faith. However, when we say that it comes ‚Äúbefore‚Äù saving faith, it is important to remember that they usually come so close together that it will ordinarily seem to us that they are happening at the same time. As God addresses the effective call of the gospel to us, he regenerates us, and we respond in faith and repentance to this call. So from our perspective, it is hard to tell any difference in time, especially because regeneration is a spiritual work that we cannot perceive with our eyes or even understand with our minds.

Scripture speaks clearly of our inability to understand things from God without His Spirit in us (1 Cor 2:14; Rom 3:11). For us to understand the things of God - to become Christians and enter the kingdom God - Scripture says that we first need to be born again and drawn by God (John 3:5; John 6:44, 65; Acts 16:14; 1 John 5:1). Regeneration enables saving faith.

Can a person be regenerated and show no evidence in his life? Support your answer from Scripture. ¬ß

It is impossible for someone who has been regenerated to not show evidence of their regeneration in their life. Regeneration is a rebirth into a new life. Those who have been born again and have come from death into life are a new creation which is different to what was before (2 Cor 5:17). A completely new creation will have different behaviours, actions, and intentions. The old self was subject to sin and made a habit of practising sin (Col 3:9-10), but the new self cannot keep on sinning in this way according to God‚Äôs word (1 John 3:9). This is because it is God‚Äôs Spirit which has caused the rebirth, therefore, the new creation is empowered by that which is God and becomes like God. This results in fruits (behaviours) in one‚Äôs life which imitate the characteristics of God instead of someone who is dead in sin and lives according to the desires of their flesh (Gal 5:19-23).

When faced with this knowledge, it becomes impossible to affirm that one who has been born again would not show any evidence in their life. It would be like affirming that an apple tree can grow watermelons. An apple tree is created to grow apples and it is by this fruit that you know the tree is an apple tree. In the same way, a person who has been regenerated has been created with God‚Äôs Spirit in them which causes them to be more like God and to practice righteousness (1 John 2:29) instead of habitual sinning like their old selves, which was a different creation. The Bible tells us that it is by this outward evidence that we are able to tell if someone really has been born again (Matt 7:15-20).

The outward evidence spoken of here is not seen merely in works like prophesy, miracles, or casting out of demons. For these works do not point to an inward change of the heart and without love at their core, they are empty and unaccepted by God (Matt 7:22-23; 1 Cor 13:1-3). The outward evidence of a regenerated person is that of a genuine, Christlike love for God (1 John 4:7); an ability to overcome the world and its temptations (1 John 5:3-4); choosing not to practice sin and having protection from Satan (1 John 5:18). The language used by the apostle John in these verses (and throughout 1 John) emphasize that these are necessary results in the lives of those who are born again. If there is genuine regeneration in a person‚Äôs life, he or she will believe that Jesus is the Christ, will refrain from a life pattern of continual sin, will love his or her brother and sister, will overcome the temptations of the world, and will be kept safe from ultimate harm by the evil one. These passages show that it is impossible for a person to be regenerated and not become truly converted.
